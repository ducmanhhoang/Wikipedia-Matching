0The Seamless Robe of Jesus (or "Holy Robe", Holy Tunic, Holy Coat of Trier, Holy Coat of Trèves) is the robe said to have been worn by Jesus during (or shortly before) his crucifixion.
1The relic is preserved in the Cathedral of Trier in Germany.
2According to the Gospel of John, the soldiers who crucified Jesus did not divide his tunic after crucifying him, but cast lots to determine who would keep it because it was woven in one piece, without seam.
3A distinction is made in the New Testament Greek between the “Then the soldiers, when they had crucified Jesus, took His garments (ta himatia) and divided them into four parts, to every soldier a part, and the coat (kai ton chitona).
4Now the coat was without seam, woven whole from the top down.
5Therefore, they said among themselves, let us not tear it, but cast lots for it, whose it will become.
6Thus the saying in Scripture was fulfilled: they divided My raiment (ta imatia) among them, and upon My vesture (epi ton himatismon) did they cast lots” (John 19:23-24; quoting the Septuagint version of Psalm 21 :18-19).
7Western traditionAccording to legend Helena, mother of Constantine the Great discovered the seamless robe in the Holy Land in the year 327 or 328 along with several other relics, including the True Cross.
8According to different versions of the story, she either bequeathed it or sent it to the city of Trier, where Constantine had lived for some years before becoming emperor.
9(The monk Altmann of Hautvillers wrote in the 9th century that Helena was born in that city, though this report is strongly disputed by most modern historians.)
10The history of the Trier robe is certain only from the 12th century.
11On May 1, 1196, Archbishop Johann I of Trier consecrated an altar in which the seamless robe was contained.
12It is no longer possible to determine the exact historical path that the robe took to arrive there, so that many hold it to be a medieval forgery.
13The various attempts at preservation and restoration through the centuries have made it difficult to determine how much of the relic (if genuine) actually stems from the time of Jesus.
14A scientific examination of the specimen has not been conducted.
15The stigmatist Therese Neumann of Konnersreuth declared that the Trier robe was authentic.Albert P Schimberg.
16The Story of Therese Neumann.

18p.14The relic is normally kept folded in a reliquary and cannot be directly viewed by the faithful.
19In 1512, during an Imperial Diet Emperor Maximilian I demanded to see the Holy Robe which was kept in the Cathedral.
20Archbishop Richard von Greiffenklau arranged the opening of the altar that had enshrined the tunic since the building of the Dome and exhibited it.
21The people of Trier heard about that and demanded to see the Holy Robe.
22Subsequently pilgrimages took place at irregular intervals to view the garment: 1513, 1514, 1515, 1516, 1517, 1524, 1531, 1538, 1545, 1655, 1810, 1844, 1891, 1933, 1959, 1996.
23The 1844 exhibition of the relic, on the instructions of Wilhelm Arnoldi, Bishop of Trier, led to the formation of the German Catholics (Deutschkatholiken), a schismatic sect formed in December of that year under the leadership of Johannes Ronge.
24The last exhibition of the tunic, in 1996, was seen by over one million pilgrims and visitors.
25Since than the Bishopric of Trier conducts the “Heilig-Rock-Tage”, an annually ten-day religious festival - but the Holy Robe is not visible during this time.
26The next pilgrimage with the exhibition of the robe is going to take place in Trier between April 13 - May 13.

29ArgenteuilAccording to the other tradition, the Empress Irene made a gift of the seamless robe to Charlemagne in about the year 800.
30Charlemagne gave it to his daughter Theocrate, abbess of Argenteuil, where it was preserved in the church of the Benedictines.
31In 1793, the parish priest, fearing that the robe would be desecrated in the French Revolution, cut the robe into pieces and hid them in separate places.
32Only four of the pieces remain.
33They were moved to the present church of Argenteuil in 1895.
34The earliest document referring to the robe at Argenteuil dates from 1156, written by Archbishop Hugh of Rouen.
35He described it, however, as the garment of the child Jesus.
36A long-running dispute claims that the Argenteuil cloth is actually not the seamless robe worn by Jesus during the crucifixion, but the garments woven for him by the Virgin Mary and worn his entire life.
37Advocates of the theory that the Argenteuil cloth is the seamless robe claim that the Trier robe is actually Jesus's mantle.
38Eastern traditionThe Eastern Orthodox Church has also preserved a tradition regarding the clothing of Jesus which was divided among the soldiers after the crucifixion.According to the tradition of the Georgian Orthodox Church, the chiton was acquired by a Jewish Rabbi from Georgia named Elioz (Elias), who was present in Jerusalem at the time of the crucifixion and bought the robe from a soldier.
39He brought it with him when he returned to his native town of Mtskheta, Georgia, where it is preserved to this day beneath a crypt in the Patriarchal Svetitskhoveli Cathedral.
40The feast day in honor of the “Chiton of the Lord” is celebrated on October 1.
41A portion of the himation was also brought to Georgia, but it was placed in the treasury of the Svetitskhoveli Cathedral, where it remained until the seventeenth century.
42Then the Persian Shah Abbas I, when he invaded Georgia, carried off the robe.
43In order to ingratiate himself with Tsar Michael Feodorovich, the Shah sent the robe as a gift to Patriarch Philaret (1619–1633) and Tsar Michael in 1625.
44The authenticity of the robe was attested by Nectarius, Archbishop of Vologda, by Patriarch Theophanes of Jerusalem and by Joannicius the Greek.
45Reports also circulated at that time of miraculous signs being worked through the relic.
46Later, two portions of the robe were taken to Saint Peterburg (the capital of the Russian Empire at the time): one in the cathedral at the Winter Palace, and the other in Sts.
47Peter and Paul Cathedral.
48A portion of the Robe was also preserved at the Cathedral of the Dormition in Moscow, and small portions at Kiev’s Sophia Cathedral, at the Ipatiev monastery near Kostroma and at certain other old temples.
49The Russian Orthodox Church commemorates the Placing of the Honorable Robe of the Lord at Moscow on July 10 (July 25 N.S.).
50At Moscow annually on that day, the robe is solemnly brought out of the chapel of the Apostles Peter and Paul at the Dormition cathedral, and it is placed on a stand for veneration by the faithful during the divine services.
51After the Divine Liturgy the robe is returned to its former place.
52Traditionally, on this day the propers chanted are of “the Life-Creating Cross”, since the day on which the relic was actually placed was the Sunday of the Cross, during Great Lent of 1625.
