9The latter statement was true at the time, especially for the aircraft of small size.

15In the course of the first tests of the fins gave rise, at high speeds drag-and-drop in the take-off phase, the phenomenon of cavitation as soon as it emerged from under the profile of the water, thus creating serious phenomena of instability.
16Performed in a brilliant way, he thought to take advantage of this phenomenon accentuandolo so as to give more support to the take-off phase, and eliminating the contact of the propeller as the marina as soon as that area was free to rotate; to do so took advantage of the boost to rise up to the surface of the water that the fins submerged, generated with the increase of the speed.
17In the end, he himself wrote, the secret is in the design of the fins that act like stones thrown without bouncing on the surface of the water.
18The P. c. 7 was the latest evolution of the series of seaplanes Piaggio, which began with the P. C. 1.
19Designed by the engineer Giovanni Pegna adopted technical solutions are very innovative.From a standstill, the P. c. 7 was similar to a seaplane to the main hull, as the function of flotation was entrusted to the fuselage, fitted with two watertight bulkheads.
20In this situation, the propeller remained partially submerged.There was a second small propeller, which is placed in the terminal part of the aircraft, with the function of making possible the maneuvers and the decolloPiaggio P. 7 in Virtual Aircraft Museum.
21For the test, were used numerous propellers.To the front, three-blade, they were ordered three variable pitch, stainless steel, made from Standard Steel.
22The other three helices, made of aluminum and also three-blade, were made by Caproni.
23The latter adopted three different fixed steps.
24The tail rotor, two-bladed and variable pitch was also made of aluminum.Under the fuselage there were three fins, two in the front, between the muzzle and wings, and a third in the retracted position, near to the propeller in the rear.
25These flaps were the subject of intense study by Pegna, who performed a number of tests with scale models in order to reach the ideal design.FIAT would have had to provide both the transmission and the engine, the latter with a power of 1 000 HP, but withdrew from the project.
26He opted then for an engine Isotta Fraschini, a V6 from a 800 HP, which was limited to 2 600 rpm/min.
27The aircraft was built in wood, with a broad use of the plywood.
28In addition to the fuselage, wings and ailerons were sealed.
29To take-off, the pilot pulled the prop back.
30Reached a certain speed, the wings lifting the aircraft, in a manner similar to a hydrofoil.
31At this point, the pilot pulled on the helix root, which had to do unplug the P. c. 7 from the surface of the water.In the phase of take-off clutches, one for each propeller, had a delicate role.
32Working together, transferring gradually the power from the propeller back to front.
33For ditching, even at high speed, the flaps would have had to ensure a positive pressure sufficient to avoid tipping.
34The technical problems and the abbandonoNel 1929 test pilot Tommaso Dal Molin, (silver medal to the value of the aircraft) was able to run some tests, using only the propeller in the rear.
35The aircraft, in this condition, had a behavior similar to a hydrofoil.
36The fins, which remained submerged, kept the muzzle of the P. C. 7 above the surface of the water; not even managing never, during the tests performed, to make a true take-off.
37The friction that was acting on the propeller back showed various problems during engagement, including abundant oil from leaking inside the fuselage.
38In these situations, the engine lost power, and was missing the thrust enough to keep lifting the aircraft.The technical problems, which were never fully resolved, led to the abandonment of project and termination of the contract by the R. Aeronautica.
