0It is native to southern Europe (but adventive in more northern countries of Europe), north Africa and southwest Asia, where it is found on neglected, damp patches of land.
1The specific epithet damascena relates to Damascus in Syria.
2The plant's common name comes from the flower being nestled in a ring of multifid, lacy bracts.
3It is also sometimes called devil-in-the-bush.
4It grows to tall, with pinnately divided, thread-like, alternate leaves.
5The flowers, blooming in early summer, are most commonly different shades of blue, but can be white, pink, or pale purple, with 5 to 25 sepals.
6The actual petals are located at the base of the stamens and are minute and clawed.
7The sepals are the only colored part of the perianth.
8The four to five carpels of the compound pistil have each an erect style.
9The fruit is a large and inflated capsule, growing from a compound ovary, and is composed of several united follicles, each containing numerous seeds.
10This is rather exceptional for a member of the buttercup family.
11The capsule becomes brown in late summer.
12The plant self-seeds, growing on the same spot year after year.
13CultivationThis easily grown plant has been a familiar subject in English cottage gardens since Elizabethan times, admired for its ferny foliage, spiky flowers and bulbous seed-heads.
14It is now widely cultivated throughout the temperate world, and numerous cultivars have been developed for garden use.
15The most common variety is 'Miss Jekyll' which has blue flowers, but the more recent 'Persian Jewels' is a mixture of white, pink, lavender and blue flowers. '
16Persian Rose' is pale pink.
17Other cultivars are 'Albion', 'Blue Midget', 'Cambridge Blue', 'Mulberry Rose', and 'Oxford Blue'. '
18Dwarf Moody Blue' is around high.
19The white-flowered cultivar 'Miss Jekyll Alba' has gained the Royal Horticultural Society's Award of Garden Merit.
20Related speciesThe related Nigella sativa (and not N. damascena) is the source of the spice variously known as nigella, kalonji or black cumin.
