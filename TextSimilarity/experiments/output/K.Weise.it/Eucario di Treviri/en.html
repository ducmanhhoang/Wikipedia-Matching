Article: Eucario di Treviri, en

0Saint Eucharius is venerated as the first bishop of Trier.
1He lived in the second half of the third century.
2According to an ancient legend, he was one of the seventy-two disciples of Christ, and was sent to Gaul by Saint Peter as bishop, together with the deacon Valerius and the subdeacon Maternus, to preach the Gospel.They came to the Rhine and to Elegia (Ehl) in Alsace, where Maternus died.
3His two companions hastened back to St. Peter and begged him to restore the dead man to life.
4St. Peter gave his pastoral staff to Eucharius, and, upon being touched with it, Maternus, who had been in his grave for forty days, returned to life.
5The Gentiles were then converted in large numbers.
6After founding many churches the three companions went to Trier where the work of evangelization progressed so rapidly that Eucharius chose that city for his episcopal residence.
7Among other miracles related in the legend he raised a dead person to life.
8An angel announced to him his approaching death and pointed out Valerius as his successor.
9Eucharius died on December 8, having been bishop for twenty-five years, and was interred in the church of St. John outside the city.Valerius was bishop for fifteen years and was succeeded by Maternus, who had in the meantime founded the dioceses of Cologne and Tongeren, being bishop altogether for forty years.
10The staff of St. Peter, with which he had been raised to life, was preserved at Cologne till the end of the tenth century when the upper half was presented to Trier, and was afterwards taken to Prague by Emperor Charles IV.
11Veneration and traditionsIn the Middle Ages it was believed that the pope used no crozier, because St. Peter had sent his episcopal staff to St. Eucharius; Innocent III concurs in this opinion (De Sacrif.
12Missæ, I, 62).
13The same instance, however, is related of several other alleged disciples of St. Peter, and later criticism interprets the staff as the distinctive mark of an envoy, especially of a missionary.
14Missionaries in subsequent centuries, e. g. St. Boniface, were occasionally called ambassadors of St. Peter, the pope who sent them being the successor of Peter.
15Moreover, in medieval times the foundation of a diocese was often referred to as early a date as possible, in order thereby to increase its reputation, perhaps also its rights.
16Thus Paris gloried in Dionysius Areopagita as its first bishop; similarly ancient origins were claimed by other Frankish dioceses.
17In time, especially through the ravages of the Normans, the more reliable earlier accounts were lost.
18When at a later period the lives of primitive holy founders, e. g. the saints of ancient Trier, came to be written anew, the gaps in tradition were filled out with various combinations and fanciful legends.
19In this way there originated in the monastery of St. Matthias near Trier the famous chronicle of Trier (Gesta Treverorum, ed.
20Waitz in Mon.
21Germ.
22Hist.; script., VIII, 111-174) in which there is a curious mixture of truth and error.
23It contains the account of the life of St. Eucharius given above.
24An amplification thereof, containing the lives of the three saints in question, is said to have been written by the monk Goldscher or Golscher, who lived in that monastery about the year 1130.
25From the "Gesta" the narrative passed unchallenged into numerous medieval works.
26Later criticism has detected many contradictions and inaccuracies in these ancient records, and it is almost universally believed at present that, with few exceptions, the first Christian missionaries came to Gaul, to which Trier then belonged, not earlier than about 250.
27Following Hontheim, Calmet and others, the Bollandists, with Marx, Aloys Lütolf, and other historians refer these bishops of Trier to a period following 250, though not all of them consider this as fully established.
28The feast of St. Eucharius is celebrated on 8 December.
