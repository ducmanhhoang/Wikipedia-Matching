0The Piaggio P.7, also known as the Piaggio-Pegna P.c.7, was an Italian racing seaplane designed and built by Piaggio for the 1929 Schneider Trophy race.
1Design and developmentSeeking to avoid the aerodynamic drag induced by floats in seaplanes of floatplane design, Ing Giovanni Pegna of the Piaggio company designed a very unusual seaplane to represent Italy in the 1929 Schneider Trophy race.
2A cantilever shoulder-wing monoplane, known both as the Piaggio P.7 and the Piaggio-Pegna P.c.7, his design floated up to its wings on its long, slender, watertight fuselage with the wings resting on the water, and employed twin high-incidence hydrofoils to get itself off the water during takeoff runs.Vašiček, Per.
3Aviation History, September 2002, p. 35.
4Sources differ on the P.7s engine; it is described both as an Isotta-Fraschini Special V6 rated at and as an Isotta-Fraschini AS-5 of .
5Vašiček, p. 35.
6The engine was connected both to a two-bladed automatic variable-pitch tractor propeller by a long metal shaft and by another shaft to a smaller marine propeller, similar to those used on motorboats, mounted beneath the aircraft's tail.
7To take off, the pilot would start the engine with the flight propeller feathered and the normal carburettor air intake closed and use a clutch to engage the tail propeller and get the aircraft moving through the water.
8The two hydrofoils, mounted beneath the fuselage on struts just forward of the wings similar to the way in which floats were mounted on floatplanes, would cause the P.7 to rise out of the water almost immediately.
9After the aircraft had risen on its hydrofoils and the flight propeller had cleared the water, the pilot would open the carburettor air e, again employing the clutch to disengage the marine propeller, and use another clutch to engage the flight propeller, which automatically would switch from feathered to flight pitch.
10Driven by its flight propeller, the aircraft then would engage in a conventional takeoff, riding on its submerged hydrofoils until it reached takeoff speed.Without the aerodynamic drag induced by floats or the weight they added to the aircraft, Pegna projected that the P.7 would reach high speeds.
11Sources differ on the speeds he predicted, claiming both 580 km/h (360 mph) and 700 km/h (434.7 mph).
12Vašiček, p. 35.
13Piaggio manufactured one P.7 and turned it over to the Italian Schneider Trophy racing team.
14Although some pilots refused to fly the aircraft, the Italian Schneider team{{'}}s Tommaso Dal Molin conducted some water tests on Lake Garda in northern Italy.
15The spray the hydroplanes generated made it difficult to see during takeoff, and persistent problems with both clutches ensued.
16The aircraft never became airborne.Not ready in time, the P.7 was excluded from the 1929 Schneider Trophy race, in which a Macchi M.52R and two Macchi M.67 seaplanes represented Italy.
17Piaggio and Pegna abandoned plans to build a second P.7.
