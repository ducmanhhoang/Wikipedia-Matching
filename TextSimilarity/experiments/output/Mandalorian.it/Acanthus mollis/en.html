0Acanthus mollis, commonly known as bear's breeches, sea dock, bearsfoot or oyster plant, is a herbaceous perennial plant with an underground rhizome in the genus Acanthus.
1It is regarded as an invasive species in some jurisdictions.Weeds of Australia, Biosecurity Queensland edition: Bear's breeches, Acanthus mollis EtymologyThe name of the genus derives from the Greek term , akanthos, the name of the plant in Greek; it was imitated in Corinthian capitals; the word is related to , akantha meaning "thorn" referring to the thorn-bearing sepals, or any thorny or prickly plant in Greek, while the Latin name of the species, mollis meaning "soft", "smooth", refers to the texture of the leaves.
2, Description Acanthus mollis reaches on average of height, with a maximum of , inflorescence included.
3It has basal clusters of deeply lobed and cut, shiny dark green leaves, soft to the touch, up to long and broad, with a long petiole.
4The inflorescence is a cylindrical spike long and can produce up to 120 flowers.
5The flowers are tubular, whitish, and lilac or rose in colour.
6Each flower is up to long and it is surrounded by three green or purplish bracts.
7The central bract is spiny and larger than the other two.
8The calyx has two lips: the upper is purple on top, rather long and forms a kind of "helmet" on top of the corolla.
9The corolla is reduced to a white lower lip, trilobed, with purple-pink venation.
10The four stamens are fused to the corolla and look like tiny brushes.
11This species flowers in late spring or early summer, from May through August.
12ReproductionAcanthus mollis is entomophilous and it is pollinated only by bees or bumble bees large enough to force their way between the upper sepal and the lower, so that they can reach the nectar at the bottom of the tube.
13The fruit is an ovoid capsule containing two to four large black seeds.
14The dispersal of seeds is by the wind (anemochory).
15These plants are usually propagated from tubers and tend to form large, localized clumps which can survive for several decades and look statuesque when well-grown, but its suitability as a garden plant is lessened on account of its invasive nature (new plants are produced readily both from seed and portions of root) and its susceptibility to powdery mildew and attacks from snails.
16DistributionThis plant is native to the Mediterranean region from Portugal and northwest Africa east to Croatia and it is one of the earliest cultivated species.
17HabitatThis garden plant is also quite common in the wild.
18It grows in dry areas, roadsides and wastelands, especially in the rocky and bushy places.
19It is tolerant of drought and shade and generally does not exceed an altitude of above sea level.
20CultureThe leaves of this plant are generally considered by historians to have been the design inspiration for the Corinthian order capitals of Greco-Roman architecture.
